About Vaccination

Vaccination is the process of introducing a weakened or inactive form of a pathogen (like a virus or bacteria) into the body to stimulate an immune response and build protection against a specific disease. This process, also known as immunization, helps the body develop antibodies and immune memory, enabling it to fight off future infections from that pathogen. Vaccines are a safe and effective way to prevent serious and potentially deadly diseases.

Vaccines are biological preparations that help protect individuals from infectious diseases by stimulating the immune system to recognize and combat pathogens. They are safe, cost-effective, and vital for global health security.

Vaccination prevents 3.5–5 million deaths annually from diseases like diphtheria, tetanus, pertussis, influenza, and measles. Beyond individual protection, it promotes herd immunity, protecting those who cannot be vaccinated.

Categories of Vaccines

Live-Attenuated

Weakened form of the virus. Provides strong immunity. (e.g., MMR)

Inactivated

Dead virus. Safe for most people. (e.g., Hepatitis A)

Subunit & Conjugate

Parts of the virus or bacteria. Fewer side effects. (e.g., HPV, Hep B)

mRNA

New tech. Triggers immune response via mRNA. (e.g., Pfizer COVID-19)

Viral Vector

Uses a harmless virus to deliver genetic info. (e.g., AstraZeneca)

Vaccines & Medicines

BCG

Protects against tuberculosis (TB).

MMR

Measles, Mumps, Rubella combined vaccine.

HPV

Prevents human papillomavirus and related cancers.

Hepatitis B

Protects against hepatitis B infection and liver disease.

DTP

Combination vaccine against diphtheria, tetanus, pertussis.

COVID-19 Vaccines

Includes Pfizer, Moderna, Covaxin, Covishield.

History of Vaccination

1796

Edward Jenner develops smallpox vaccine using cowpox.

1885

Pasteur introduces rabies vaccine.

1955

Salk polio vaccine is licensed.

1980

WHO declares smallpox eradicated worldwide.

2006

HPV vaccine introduced for cervical cancer prevention.

2020

COVID-19 vaccines developed using mRNA and viral vector tech.

New Researches

Modern vaccination research focuses on improving delivery, increasing immunity, and developing vaccines for complex diseases.

  • Universal flu vaccine (NIH, Sanofi)
  • mRNA-based cancer vaccines
  • DNA vaccines for infectious diseases
  • Oral and nasal vaccines for needle-free delivery
  • AI-powered antigen prediction
